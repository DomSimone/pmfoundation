Defense of sovereignty and achieving military success is well within the rights of all nations. However, we have a collective standard that all progressive and civilized societies have subscribed to. It's in the best interest for Africa as a collective to consider the very present threat of international events spilling over into our realm. There are a few vulnerable spots which we are yet to decisively resolve in restoration towards collective harmony. From the unspoken yet consistent conflict in Northern Nigeria, to the ever evolving battle between the DRC and Rwanda, and the unseen yet felt anxiety as far as the arrangement of the Chagos Islands' sovereignty, Regional and continental cooperation have to set precedence as there is no other credible and potent standard for international law than our own internal moral standard.

The reality of things as they stand, is that the International order has collapsed and there is an ongoing clashing of the titans to determine whose agenda will set the new world order. While in world War 2 the line was very easy to draw and 2 sides were in play; currently there are at least 3 big blocks of which none has the outright superiority. These include the Western Hemisphere characterized by NATO, BRICS countries, and OPEC countries. All have a leverage that can't be outright isolated. Thus the negotiating table, once the dust settles, will require a significantly more robust and flexible class of negotiations.

No one is going to get everything they want, and Africa must prepare for this reality. We've advocated for decades to be more embedded in the permanent infrastructure of international multilateral forums, particularly the United Nations. The body itself is now confronted with the reality that its present infrastructure cannot fully house the challenges of the 21st century global village. If it will survive the bold disregard for international order by the giant countries, it will have to be cognizant of the developing countries' maturing as far as ability to engage and inevitably save the systems of the United Nations. This needs its own article for full expansion.

The quintessential statesman/states stateswoman is in high demand. The high speed real-time challenges of the world today need a robust and impersonal class of diplomat who know that things won't entirely go their way; however their biggest success will be in that they preserved civilization for generations to come at the sacrifice of their personal gratification and memory insert.

Peace to all

It has to start somewhere, if it must go anywhere at all: Race Politics

Racial politics is counterproductive to any form of progress within the economic empowerment and growth context of our Nation; no matter the part of the spectrum you are judging it form.

Racial politics is counterproductive to any form of progress within the economic empowerment and growth context of our Nation; no matter the part of the spectrum you are judging it form.

The far right use racial identity politics to simplify macroeconomic concepts of state intervention in the economy, giving fear to the middle class that their property and assets would be part of the overall national program to alleviate poverty. Actual reading of policy documents, and study of the history of state intervention in uplifting a people in South Africa, would show that if executed with respect for fundamental economic principle, which involves state injection of capital raised through tax and exchange of corporate social responsibility subsidy for communal equity share in conglomerate ownership (whose main industry participants are already publicly traded by nature on the stock exchange), the marginal utility of previously unserviceable peoples could be improved upon .

The far left uses racial politics to eliminate the burden of performance on the current regime (political and economic), and more affluent members of previously disenfranchised groups, to own up to the fact that there is much more that could be done right now without having to recite a history that is much more complicated than the political liberation solution will reveal. Economic segregation was not cut and dry to the total seclusion of disadvantaged groups. There were people of colour who flourished under apartheid laws, and if you're to look at the demographics of how many people are better off now from the previously disadvantaged groups; the calculus will show that the status quo is the same, and even protected by virtue of the smoke screen of racially based affirmative action.

Passive Moderates utilize race to give a pause on all fronts, with the impression that we are facing a 300 year old problem, and so we can't be expected to solve it. And furthermore state that, it's just too many people with too few resources. In reality, if applied appropriately, the tools of public and private sector can start an intensive drive to raise the basic standard of living to eliminate extreme poverty; whose existence in 21st century South Africa cannot be considered humane.

We have to be frank with ourselves if we're to really do anything that matters. It can't be right that in this day and age, poverty is so acceptable that we can bicker about something we didn't choose. We didn't choose our color or gender. But we can choose our economic destiny as a nation. Front and center of this is a non-racial and non-sexist South Africa. We can't wait for everything to be right to apply the principle. The application of the principle is what will lead to the restoration of dignity to all peoples.

Let's not forget, it was this very distinction that caused the mess in the first place. We can't clean the house with the same mud that has sullied it.

Everyone matters, no matter the race or language. As long as Africa is your home, you belong here; and thus you have the right to thrive, and the responsibility to not accept the indignation of the world on your neighbor, and nation.

PM Reflections: Africa’s Role In The New World Order

2 years into a new decade, it is safe to assert that we are in a new word order. The global elements of power brokering and influence have been decentralized dramatically by the development of globalized free speech mechanisms such as social media and independent internet news and ideology sources.

2 years into a new decade, it is safe to assert that we are in a new word order. The global elements of power brokering and influence have been decentralized dramatically by the development of globalized free speech mechanisms such as social media and independent internet news and ideology sources. Furthermore; the focus of the global village engaging in conflicts whose roots can be traced to the last great shift in world order post world war 2 provides a fertile ground for a continent such as Africa to define it’s own destiny should the right motivation and strategy be applied.

Africa became independent in a period of conflict between two centers of power. This has caused for a majority of its birthing years to be a manifestation of proxy dreams and aspirations for the aforementioned powers through the proxy wars and political destabilization exercises for regimes to be formed in the image of the dreams of others. Africa, for its formative years has had a disadvantage that other continents didn’t have in their own infant stages.

Globalization made it impossible for the African organic development of political, ideological and economic systems as the veil of seclusion was not afforded to us. Europe upon liberation from the Roman empire had a season of the dark ages, along with inquisitions and purges based on religion, race and culture. This inevitably led to the development of enlightenment, humanism and many other liberal spheres of thought as we know them today. The Russian empire underwent a period of purges in which millions were sacrificed in order to create the utopia of Socialist revolution as per the fathers of the movement. These purges are as early as the 20th century.

The American continent was also engaged in an unsavory campaign of conquest in the attaining of territory to be amalgamated into what we know today as the United States of America. So detached was the American continent, that it practiced the policy of laissez-faire (the policy of leaving things to take their own course, without interfering) as a response to the conflicts of Europe in the scramble for territory in colonies and within the continent. Such was this policy that the Americans joined both word wars only after a direct provocation was insinuated, and not necessarily at the call of their allies.

China has the cultural revolution under Chairman Mao’s leadership, in which China excluded itself from the global village to work out its identity and worldview. It remains a culture of the global power to keep its cards close to its chest so as to determine what and who is influencing Chinese society and development.

Africa, on the other hand was expected to mature immediately after attaining independence. This has led to the unwarranted burden of being perfect in practice of democracy, economics and cultural relations. The burden imposed has made apathy of the African towards his/her future to be a more comfortable view than the choice to labour through the birth pains of a new nation.

With this said; it is important to note that there has been opportunities to develop strong institution whose function in ensuring a collective approach of African development to be easier to implement. The formation of the African Union, along with the Regional Economic Communities, has ensured a platform from which we as a continent can determine our own destiny if the right approach is applied.

The last decade has seen the development of numerous instruments by which Africa can apply itself towards internal and collective development at a pace that is organic when the right attitude is applied to the protocols adopted. Chief of these is the Agenda 2063 initiative. At its formation, the main criticism was one of impatience; citing that 50 years is too long a period to look at when it comes to development. Given my aforementioned analysis of development of the global powers, I dare say we have an advantage that they didn’t have, in the sense that we have clearly stated what we want Africa to become by the time 2063 arrives. The other regions of the world have accidentally arrived where they are based on organic events and opportunity met.

We however have a clear and concise idea of what we want to have achieved, as it is summed up in the following 7 Aspirations:

1. A prosperous Africa based on inclusive growth and sustainable development

2. An integrated continent, politically united based on the ideals of Pan Africanism and the vision of Africa’s Renaissance

3. An Africa of good governance, democracy, respect for human rights, justice and the rule of law

4. A peaceful and secure Africa

5. An Africa with a strong cultural identity, common heritage, values and ethics

6. An Africa, whose development is people-driven, relying on the potential of African people, especially its women and youth, and caring for children

7. Africa as a strong, united, resilient and influential global player and partner

As simple and direct as this text may appear at first glance, when applied to the usual events in the evolution of civilizations, such a clear vision is unique and enviable. Considering that for most of its free life, the majority of its territories has been a stage for proxy conflicts; the temerity to unite towards creating a united development identity is in itself a feat other regions struggle with to this day.

We have faults, and we have weaknesses. However, the main aim of this article is to reassert that it is normal for civilizations to develop with these weaknesses being in the culture of the era. It is however up to us as citizens and leaders to determine whether the future of our continent is worth the labor of optimism and endurance, even if we ourselves will not be around to enjoy the fruits of our labour.

As the North Atlantic world is rekindling the battling a bipolar conflict between East and West, Africa is provided with the opportunity to evolve based on terms which we can define for ourselves. We can be one of the chief beneficiaries of these circumstances if only we develop a collective domestic and global policy that prioritizes our own interests in whichever resolution and alliance which we support as events unfold.

To this end, I seek to propose the following considerations upon which we must engage both our internal as well as global interactions should we have a chance to achieve the Africa we want in spite of the fragmenting of the global village:

1. Africa must ensure its political systems are secured and safe from proxy war vulnerabilities

The main trait of proxy wars is that they are made possible by weak democratic institutions and regional cooperation towards supporting one another in strengthening these organs of state. Military coups and unfair election processes are factors which contribute to a fear and paranoia that can be utilized to force countries into allegiances that aren’t of organic or ideological convictions.

It is therefore essential that Africa makes use of the instruments which are in place to ensure remedies that are swift and effective in resolving conflict. Some of these instruments include:

- The African Charter on Democracy, Elections & Governance (ACDEG)

- The Peace & Security Council, with its powers to intervene in matters of stability and security in the continent

- Regional Economic Communities remedies for Democratic solutions e.g. Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) in enforcing election results in the event of a standoff between concerned parties; as well as deployment of regional peacekeeping missions in the event of civil military conflict.

The concept of sovereignty must be applied in Africa with a very clear understanding that as much as our borders are official, our bond as a people has no border. As such, respectful intervention to ensure that none of our countries are isolated and vulnerable to external elements and interests is paramount.

2. Our Economic Model of Market participation ought to aspire towards internal goods production and intra-trade as priority

The colonial economic model of African contribution to global markets was of resource extraction for goods to be manufactured in their realms for sale back to the world and Africa itself. As such, African infrastructure was built solely for the ease of export and not for the internal economic development of the indigenous African. The African Union, through its different organs, has developed tools to address this matter effectively. Two of these, which I consider salient to the domestication of market capitalization, are:

- The African Continental Free Trade Area: A free trade area agreement upon which signatories have taken it upon themselves to place priority on African goods and services in the considerations of trade and industrial strategies. This provides an important obligation for countries to not only make the movement of goods and peoples easier, but also for the efficient and high standard test of quality in delivery of goods and services to one another. It is not a foregone conclusion that African goods and services must be acquired; however it helps in incentive to develop competitive market share participation.

- The Program for Infrastructure Development in Africa (P.I.D.A): A mechanism envisaged and spearheaded by the African Union Development Agency (AUDA), a.k.a New Partnership for African Development (NEPAD), in order to unite member states national development plans to include collective input into the development of transport, Energy, and Information Communications Technology (ICT) infrastructure; in order to ease the means by which goods and people can be facilitated for domestic economic development,and trade.

3. The African Common Position principle ought to be rekindled as a potent means of engagement with the global village

The African Continent has a mechanism by which it produces African Common Positions on matters that affect global relations. This mechanism is of highest importance now, considering the way the world is evolving. An impediment to the effectiveness of this tool has been the manipulation of bilateral channels by some of our partners, in which the result was contradiction of our common positions when voting and decision time was occurring in global forums.

It is going to take a special type of leadership to enforce this, as it requires a braveness that is aware of the possibility of underhanded tactics by some global partners to dismantle the African Common Position. As such; in our aspiration to be united, we must be resolved to defend and assist one another in the event of such tactics being attempted.

The governance and economic elements are always the first two areas of sabotage in the attempt to twist the arm of African diplomacy towards the direction of the perpetrator. We must therefore be pragmatic and cerebral concerning our own defence mechanisms in these spheres. We have a lot of areas of incentive from whence we can counter the tactics of bilateral manipulation.

African Diplomats ought to realize the effectiveness of realpolitik, along with the essential resolve that they are the first line of defense when it comes to Africa’s value in global affairs. This responsibility cannot be carried out on the basis of stagnant fundamentalism nor on the fear of the norms being disrupted. The world is changing, and African diplomacy ought to capitalize on this for a positive and profitable return to the continent. The African Common Position mechanism is a fundamental pillar in structuring our role in the current development of global affairs.

Conclusion

The state of world affairs is in a state of high volatility. With the decentralization of information sharing, along with the tool of cyber warfare, diplomacy is no longer as bipolar or secluded as once was considered. We thus ought to travel our path with reverence for the responsibility bequeathed to us to make the African Renaissance a reality. This can only be done with a spirit of boldness. We have the tools at our disposal to ensure a revival of the hearts, vision and economy of Africa. These tools will only be effective upon their application with wisdom, enthusiasm and endurance. Temerity will lead us to the Africa we want.

I thus make the call; let’s (Africa) unite in common cause for our people, our nation and our continent.

Yours in service

Patson Malisa

Last year, I had the honour of being hosted by the Network of African Youth for Development for a conversation on the relevance of Ubuntu in leadership and policy development in Africa. We as a generation need to regain such quality of service as we find ourselves in a circle of constantly meeting for the drafting of youth positions without commitment to ensuring their grassroots implementation. We are indeed at a very crucial juncture in Africa’s development. Now more than ever, we have the power in our hands to become the change that we want to see. I believe that with the embracing of the innovation of us the youth, who happen to be the majority citizens of Africa, the deliverables of Agenda 2063 will be realized. The African Youth Charter celebrates over a decade of existence; however, it is also experiencing a turning point moment as we the youth are called to champion its ideals.

Let us continue to utilize this diplomatic platform as a tool to inspire one another through hard work, dedication and determination to realize an Africa where the free movement of people, capital, goods and services will result in significant increases in trade and investments amongst African countries rising to unprecedented levels, and strengthen Africa’s place in global trade.

Moreover, I believe that the power of a united effort beyond the convention room is most important now more than ever before. We can no longer afford to work in silos. The moment we redeem ourselves from the personality and image cult and seek to do what’s “best for business”, we will see greater levels of milestones being attained.

Let us therefore approach this task before us as youths with the reverence due to it, and lean towards wisdom and consideration of the pressures at hand. We cannot afford to be apathetic and moreover, we cannot afford to marginalize one another.

We are indeed the change that Africa has been looking for. Our power is in our youth. Our motivation is change. Unity is our shield for the challenges ahead of us. Our continent awaits our manifestation. Let us assemble and strategize towards practical and meaningful initiatives for our people, for our nations and for our Africa.

Let Africa be great

In Conversation with Patson Malisa: Ubuntu & cooperation towards development in Africa

As Published in the Inspire Magazine on May 16th 2016: "Patson Malisa is a Diplomacy Advocate, Thought Leader, Executive and Facilitator..."

As Published in the Inspire Magazine on May 16th 2016:

"Patson Malisa is a Diplomacy Advocate, Thought Leader, Executive and Facilitator. He has served in national and continental youth platforms since the age of 18, advocating the convening of government and civil society to the dialogue forum for the advancing of policy for the Upliftment of youth. He serves as Acting Secretary & Head of Strategic Planning and Partnerships for the Infrastructure and Energy Cluster at the Economic, Social and Cultural Council (ECOSOCC) of the African Union. He is also President of the Organization of African Youth, and South Africa's Country Coordinator for the Network of African Youth for Development. You can tweet him @patsonmalisa

Q1. What inspires your work?

I’m mostly inspired by the rich history which we have inherited as Africans. We have inherited a great treasure as well as a great struggle. We have come a long way in terms of attaining independence and political sovereignty. We now have the challenge of empowering our people with the economic capacity to achieve their full capacity. This is a purpose which I hold in the highest of regard and honour. My motivation is drawn from the understanding of the role I must play for my generation in achieving this next phase of African upliftment.

Q2. What does the term Ubuntu mean?

Ubuntu simply translated means, “I am because you are.” This is a principle which has its origin amongst the native African tribes of South Africa. It is accompanied by the philosophy which states, “Umunthu ngumunthu ngabanthu.” This is translated to say, A person is a person because of the people. It is therefore the acknowledgement that the quality of our humanity is measured by the state of life being lived by the people around us.

Q3. Are there instances of Ubuntu that you can point to in your local community?

I believe that Ubuntu is best displayed when people strive to live for something bigger than themselves. In my community, this is manifested through the work of NGOs and CBOs who are advocating for the improvement of service delivery, advocating for the strengthening of crime prevention as well as the protection of women and children. Churches, Synagogues, Mosques and Temples which teach believers to live in service in humanity also promote the principle of Ubuntu. Families that raise their children to achieve something greater than themselves is also a great example of Ubuntu at work.

Q4. To what extent do Africans embrace and live according to Ubuntu?

Ubuntu is an ideal which is embraced in the fabric of African culture. At grassroots level, it is manifested in our daily lives at different levels. However, as the levels of bureaucracy rise, Ubuntu seems to be given less breathing space. International Relations is governed mostly on the principle of national interest. Therefore, because of varied trading and other relationships that are held by many of our African countries with other regions, unity for a common African position is often limited to declarations (which have internal contentions of their own) and rarely escalates to implementation. Our generation has the task of settling this issue effectively.

Q5. Our differences as Africans are many; how can we use this for the good of the continent?

We have to accept each other’s differences as complimentary factors to our collective development. No one community has all it needs to survive. Therefore, it is important to embrace a principle of unity in diversity and allow each good element of our rich society to manifest itself and yield profit for all. Best practices at social, political and economic levels will be edified through this enriching exercise.

Q6. What’s the role of the AU in promoting cooperation and development in Africa?

The African Union is the bridge upon which African States can reach a place of common position of inter-regional and global cooperation. This helps to consolidate national interest into a collective interest for the strengthening of our efforts to improve our continent. This also eases the complexity of negotiating in international platforms where global decisions with regards to the direction of the world will be taken. The AU also stands as a champion for the attainment of resources which facilitate domestic trade as well as enhancing our export markets to the globe under enriching terms for the Continent.

Q7. Is relaxing visa regulations, like Ghana just did, the answer to continental cooperation?

It is definitely the start of a great initiative to enhance inter-regional trade. It is a shame that in many African countries, it is easier for a person from another continent to travel to them than it is for neighbouring countries. This is a serious barrier to domestic trade and thus Ghana’s initiative is a decisive display of commitment to the values enshrined in the AU Agenda 2063 Declaration.

Q8. ”President for life” is a big issue in Africa, how can we redress this?

The promotion of democratic values which are universally agreed upon will be the best place to begin our address of this issue. Democracy is practiced under different time limits across the world. Therefore we need to establish an African position on our definition of just democratic process. While we have an AU Charter which urges the practice of democracy and condemns military coups and violation of election processes, there is no an agreed term limit or year cap on the term. This will need to be addressed carefully with consideration for the nature of our status of development.

Another issue which keeps heads of states in power for long is the fear of what awaits after leaving the protection of presidency. Africa has a history of persecution of former presidents by their successors as a political statement by the incumbent to appease their constituencies. Therefore a transitional package might have to be considered which prevents unjust persecutions in this regard. A good pension package would also be a good incentive.

Q9. In what practical ways can the youths be agents of Ubuntu in their local community?

The Youth are best positioned as champions for the development and exhortation of their communities. Getting involved in voluntary service which addresses local challenges to quality of life is a great starting place for advancing the principle of Ubuntu. Being involved in the political process of their communities in the form of elections and consultative forums with leaders is a responsibility of every citizen, and thus the responsible engagement of youth in this regard will definitely benefit local communities and ultimately the nations that house them.

Q1O. Last words to the young people out there:

We are on a marathon. The journey which we have embarked upon is not only for ourselves, but for the generations that will follow. Let us therefore run our race with patience as well as with hope that the ideals of a prosperous and poverty-free Africa are possible. If it’s going to be, it is up to us."

International Youth Day: Defining The Road to 2030

International Youth Day gives us the opportunity to celebrate the powerful force that is Youth in the development of our world. The statistics show that youth represent the broadest representative demographic in population figures.

International Youth Day gives us the opportunity to celebrate the powerful force that is Youth in the development of our world. The statistics show that youth represent the broadest representative demographic in population figures. Established firms that are mass consumer oriented direct their marketing to ensure that young people are attracted to their products and services. While market trends and analysis highlight young people as a notable consumer, the statistics with regards to economic participation in production and service rendering are quite discouraging.

Many of our nations, while afloat, are contracted with regards to economic opportunities. Other nations are failing to survive, and rely on limited means of trade with the world to remain afloat. Thus their industry is dependent on one sector with little to no room for the emergence of youth. Experience is often used as the gatekeeper for the entry of new blood into these entities, thus keeping a monopoly hold on the demographic that will get access to the fruits of the establishment.

These realities would very easily discourage many into the abyss of thought that there is no way out for the youth of today. For others, it is a ticket to rebellion and anarchy in the name of attaining a form of freedom without definition. However, there is a path to which our generation is called which requires the eyes and ears to be attentive to the wisdom of time. This can be found in the simple word, Vision.

Vision, in this context, refers to the perceived future/outcome of our efforts. While we have many plans of action, the question still remains, what kind of country, region and world do we intend on leaving for the next generation? Are we going to leave them with the same options we had to choose from among, or are we going to take the initiative to explore beyond the immediate possibilities and perceived limitations which we have been programmed to believe that they are immovable?

In my experience in the world of diplomacy and youth advocacy, I have seen how the youth voice has been given pacifiers to deter the masses from seeking to go beyond the boundaries set by tradition for the maintenance of perceived order and governability. It has become as good as a caged hamster in a cage running against the momentum of the wheel in the hope of overcoming the momentum. The unfortunate truth is that this momentum never turns in favour of the hamster. It only tires the poor animal until it falls off. Even so, passive acceptance of our generation’s fate being unchangeable and dooming youth diplomacy to conferences and retreats that end with the same language documents is going to leave us apathetic and derailed from the task of uplifting our people.

It is therefore with this, that I challenge the youth leaders of this network and across the spectrum to choose to think differently about the bureaucracy which we are inheriting. Let us understand its science and limitations. Let us engage the world and its mathematical equations. Let us critically assess the trends of our culture and put in the work needed to ensure that we can ensure a supply for the demand of human evolution as the population figures continue to grow. Let us bear in mind the fact that principle trade traditions that saw the pillaging of nations for the benefit of others by form of conquest and unscrupulous diplomacy has only yielded a cycle of events that keep repeating themselves in different forms. Let us be the generation that decides that this cycle stops with us.

When approaching sustainable development, let us envision our role as the generation that embraces the true spirit of globalization where national interest doesn’t trump humanity’s interest. Let us be the generation that seeks to embrace the principles of value added diplomacy, where our nations seek to trade and interact on the premise of adding value to the counterpart being engaged. This approach to diplomacy is a quintessential start to the ensuring of a world where sustainable trade relations can be established. It also ensures that the value of youth innovation and evolution of the culture is not seen as a threat, but rather as an addition to the plethora of the nation’s treasure to integrate into the commonwealth of humanity.

I leave you with the words of the father of my nation, the late great Nelson Mandela who said, “It always seems impossible until it is done.”

Happy International Youth Day

Race and the African Renaissance

Africa’s history is very much entangled with the world’s evolution. To this point, it is impossible for us to define an African vision that is isolated from its relations to the rest of the world in social, economic and political spheres of cooperation. It is therefore with this that I pose the following question to the current generation of Africans; Is our interpretation of the concept of the African Renaissance conscious of the racial diversity in the composition of African citizenry? When we state Africa to be for Africans, are we referring to Africans of black skin colour only, or are we conscious that Africans consist of European, Asian and Arabic descent among many others?

I am questioning this notion given the complexities of our history and the challenges that are before us in defining a stable future for our continent. Africa is one of very few geographic areas on earth in which the nomadic, trade evolution and territorial conquests resulted in the inescapable integration of societies and cultures from almost every part of the world. Therefore, Africa is most probably the most diverse society due to its naturalized cultural and racial composition. Therefore, when we address the means of achieving Agenda 2063 in which Africa has a strengthened position in global trade, we must address the question of whether Africa is truly at peace with its inner diversity first.

I ask also this based on my own experiences, in which I have seen marginalization in rhetoric of speeches and the caucus meetings, of would be minority groups who actually mean well in their presence at meetings for the advancement of the African Renaissance. Many times, I see minority races and people groups being bullied out of African summits and discussions with regards to the future of development. Often times it is argued by the perpetrators that the struggle for Africa’s emancipation has only been borne by a certain type of African. However, I would boldly challenge that considering the history of slavery, colonialism, genocide, concentration camps and human rights deprivation suffered by all African citizens, whether minority or majority, at some point in time in our history.

These minority groups have no alternatives for citizenship and they have their home entrenched in African soil due to generational dwelling, just like every one of us. We cannot escape the fact that history has put us in a position to challenge our understanding of what African citizenship is. It isn’t like in the pre-Common Era times where citizenship was simply a matter of bloodline or namesake. Therefore, when we speak of empowering the African, are we conscious that minority races and cultures have a right to be heard and embraced as part of achieving the African dream? Does the white and coloured Afrikaaner of South Africa have a place in our vision of the African Renaissance? After all, they have the name entrenched in their tribe. Is there a place for the English African? How about the Indian African in Mauritius or the Arab African in Egypt? How about the Jewish Africans of Ethiopia? How about the Chinese African? Do these groups, as well as the others that I haven’t mentioned, have a place in our Agenda 2063?

The main detriment of African progress has been the discrimination of people groups based on status, colour, language and birth place. If we do not have a solid respect for one another, how can we trade with one another? If we do not hold one another in high esteem, how do we prevent genocide? If we do not see the value of each African, how do we ensure quality service delivery and infrastructural development?

This issue has caused us to neglect the talent and capacity of our people many times and seek the expertise and capital of other nations at the expense of developing our domestic constituency. The truth of the matter is that International Relations is run on the principle of National Interest. Before an African is going to eat from a deal with another region, you can be guaranteed that that region will have ensured that 5 of its own citizens has eaten as a result of that deal. It is therefore essential to us as a people to realize that our biggest and most important investment partner is the African. We are blessed to have diverse cultures and understandings, and thus our world trade engagement should never be from a place of wanting but from a place of value addition.

It is my hope and dream this Africa day that we will take a hold of this great truth and maximize on the greatness that is in the fabric of our continent, and achieve the Africa we want. I believe there are enough progressives in our continent to ensure that this is realized.

Let Africa be great

Patson Malisa
